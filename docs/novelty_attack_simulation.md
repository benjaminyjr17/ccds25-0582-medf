# MEDF — Novelty Attack Simulation (Viva Defense Stress Test)
Date: 2026-02-24  
Version: 1.0.0  
Scope: Aligned to Stage 1 prompts

## 10 Examiner Attacks and Defense Lines

### Attack 1: "This is just a wrapper over existing frameworks. Where is novelty?"
Defense:
- Novelty is in cross-framework harmonisation plus stakeholder-weighted comparative scoring in one computational pipeline.
- Existing tools usually evaluate one framework at a time and do not model stakeholder conflict + compromise space together.

### Attack 2: "Your scoring seems arbitrary."
Defense:
- Stage 1 uses deterministic placeholders to validate architecture.
- Scoring contracts are stable so validated methods (TOPSIS/WSM/AHP) can be inserted without API redesign.

### Attack 3: "Why trust your stakeholder weights?"
Defense:
- Weights are explicit, auditable inputs rather than hidden assumptions.
- Stakeholder profiles are persisted and version-visible for reproducibility.

### Attack 4: "Conflict detection is weak."
Defense:
- Conflict endpoint contract is implemented now; statistical internals are staged.
- Pairwise disagreement and divergent dimensions are already represented in typed models for direct method substitution.

### Attack 5: "No scalability proof."
Defense:
- Correct for Stage 1: scope is methodological prototype.
- Layering (router/service/data) enables migration from SQLite to production DB without contract breakage.

### Attack 6: "Why not just use one governance framework?"
Defense:
- Single-framework outputs can mask normative disagreement.
- MEDF is designed precisely to expose cross-framework and cross-stakeholder divergence.

### Attack 7: "Your harmonisation mapping may bias outcomes."
Defense:
- Mapping is explicit and inspectable; no hidden ontology transforms.
- Stage 1 placeholder mapping is identity-based to reduce confounds before advanced alignment logic.

### Attack 8: "This is engineering, not research contribution."
Defense:
- Contribution target is computational formalization of multi-stakeholder ethical trade-off analysis.
- Engineering rigor is necessary to ensure reproducible research claims.

### Attack 9: "Where is empirical validation?"
Defense:
- Stage 1 establishes validated infrastructure.
- Stage 2/3 attach empirical case studies, expert validation, and comparative analysis on the same stable interfaces.

### Attack 10: "How do you defend reproducibility?"
Defense:
- Typed schemas, deterministic placeholders, YAML versioning, and seeded defaults ensure reruns are traceable.
- API docs and endpoint-level outputs provide examiner-verifiable artifacts.

## Quick-Reference Defense Matrix
- Novelty claim: cross-framework + stakeholder conflict + compromise pipeline.
- Rigor claim: typed contracts, deterministic baseline, reproducible initialization.
- Scope claim: Stage 1 validates architecture; advanced algorithms are staged integrations.
- Risk mitigation: explicit mappings, explicit weights, explicit error models.

## Rehearsal Protocol
1. 60-second system summary: problem, novelty, architecture.
2. 90-second method roadmap: Stage 1 baseline to Stage 2/3 validated algorithms.
3. 90-second reproducibility defense: deterministic behavior and schema contracts.
4. 3-minute attack drill: answer all 10 attacks with one claim + one evidence point.
5. Final check: avoid overstating Stage 1 outputs; emphasize staged validation strategy.
